A History of Sourdough

Ancient to Classical Eras

Bread and sourdough have a long and rich history dating back thousands of years. The earliest evidence of bread-making dates back to ancient civilizations in Egypt, where it was a staple food. In those times, people would mix flour and water to create a simple dough, which would then be baked on hot stones.

Sourdough, on the other hand, has been used for baking bread for even longer. It is believed that sourdough was discovered by accident when a mixture of flour and water was left to ferment for several days, creating a naturally occurring mixture of yeast and lactic acid bacteria. This mixture, also known as a sourdough starter, can be used to make bread that has a unique flavor and texture, as well as longer shelf life, compared to bread made with commercial yeast.

Medieval to Colonial Eras

The use of sourdough in bread-making spread from ancient civilizations to Europe, where it became a staple food. During the colonial era, sourdough was brought to the Americas by settlers, who used it to make bread in their new homes. Today, sourdough is still used in many parts of the world, particularly in Europe and the United States, where it is prized for its unique flavor and texture.

Modern Times

In recent years, sourdough has experienced a resurgence in popularity as more people seek out traditional, unprocessed foods. With the rise of artisanal bakeries and an increased interest in home baking, sourdough has once again become a staple in many kitchens.
